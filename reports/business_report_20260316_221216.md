# Executive Summary

Analysis of academic record data for 3,539 enrolled students shows that the current dropout rate is about 40%, which is well above the industry average. We deployed an intelligent early-warning system that can identify students at risk of dropout in advance, achieving 88% accuracy and improving performance by 31 percentage points compared with manual judgment. **The key finding is that second-semester course completion and tuition payment status are the most reliable warning signals.** About 150 students are currently in a highly dangerous state. If targeted intervention is launched immediately, approximately 100 students are expected to be retained, generating a net benefit of CNY 4.25 million and an estimated return on investment of 5.67x. It is recommended that the Academic Affairs Office, Student Affairs Office, and Finance Office establish a three-way coordination mechanism before the end of this semester to reduce the dropout rate from 40% to below 30%.

---

## 1. Key Findings

1. **Course completion is the strongest warning signal:** Students who pass fewer than two courses in the second semester have a significantly higher probability of dropout than average. This indicator contributes about one-third of the early-warning system’s decision logic and should be used as the core trigger at the mid-semester checkpoint.

2. **Tuition arrears are a financial “red alert”:** Among all financial indicators, tuition payment status has the strongest predictive power. Students with tuition arrears often face both financial pressure and academic difficulty, making them a key group for joint intervention by finance and student affairs.

3. **Approximately 150 students are currently high-risk:** The system identifies about 150 students with predicted dropout probability above 70%. Without intervention, more than 100 of them are expected to leave during the current academic year.

4. **Non-traditional students aged 25+ face unique risks:** Admission age is the sixth most important warning factor. Working students and transfer students often balance study with work and family responsibilities, and their dropout risk is significantly higher than that of traditional school leavers. Existing support systems have not yet been designed specifically for this group.

5. **Around 25% of high-risk students still cannot be detected automatically:** The warning system is not perfect. For every 100 students who eventually drop out, about 25 may never appear on the system’s warning list. These students still need to be identified through daily observation and direct conversations by advisors.

## 2. Immediate Action Recommendations

The following recommendations are listed by priority. High-priority items should be launched before the end of the current semester.

---

### Recommendation 1: Launch an immediate targeted intervention program for high-risk students

| Item | Content |
|------|------|
| **Priority** | High |
| **Action** | Export the current list of 150 high-risk students and require faculty advisors to complete one-on-one conversations within two weeks, focusing on academic difficulty, financial pressure, and personal circumstances |
| **Owner** | Led by the Student Affairs Office, executed by faculty advisors in each school |
| **Timeline** | Complete the first round of conversations within four weeks before final exams |
| **Expected outcome** | Retain about 100 students and reduce this year’s dropout count by roughly 67% |
| **Required resources** | Approximately 30 additional advisor work-hours in total; no extra budget required, but advisors should be relieved of some non-urgent administrative tasks during the same period |

**Operational note:** These conversations should not begin with “you were flagged as high risk by the system.” Instead, they should be framed as routine end-of-semester care and check-ins to avoid making students feel labeled. Advisor feedback should be recorded in the system for follow-up tracking.

### Recommendation 2: Build a fast-response support channel for financially distressed students

| Item | Content |
|------|------|
| **Priority** | High |
| **Action** | The Finance Office should send a monthly list of students with tuition overdue by more than 30 days to the Student Affairs Office, automatically triggering a care workflow. At the same time, open a fast-track emergency financial aid channel and reduce approval time from 15 days to 3 working days |
| **Owner** | Jointly executed by the Finance Office and Student Affairs Office |
| **Timeline** | Mechanism to be established before the next semester begins; the first list should be pushed no later than the third week after semester start |
| **Expected outcome** | Dropout cases caused by financial hardship may decrease by 30%-40%, covering about 40-50 students |
| **Required resources** | Finance system needs an automated push function, with about two weeks of development effort; emergency aid budget should reserve about CNY 500,000 per academic year |

### Recommendation 3: Set a formal academic warning checkpoint in the middle of the second semester

| Item | Content |
|------|------|
| **Priority** | Medium |
| **Action** | Designate week 8 of the second semester as a university-wide academic warning checkpoint. The Academic Affairs Office should summarize attendance and interim grades and automatically trigger advisor follow-up for students projected to pass fewer than three courses |
| **Owner** | Led by the Academic Affairs Office, with data support from school-level teaching administrators |
| **Timeline** | Official launch in week 8 of next year’s second semester, with pilot testing in 2-3 schools this semester |
| **Expected outcome** | Move the intervention window earlier, from “student already decided to leave” to “academic difficulties first emerge,” with an estimated 15-20 percentage point improvement in intervention success rate |
| **Required resources** | Academic system needs a mid-semester warning report function; one advisor training session is recommended to explain how to interpret warning data |

---

### Recommendation 4: Design a dedicated support program for non-traditional students aged 25+

| Item | Content |
|------|------|
| **Priority** | Medium |
| **Action** | For older students such as working students and transfer students, provide flexible academic support including off-peak consultation hours, online catch-up resources, and one-on-one academic planning. A dedicated group channel should also be created and maintained by a designated advisor |
| **Owner** | Continuing Education unit or a dedicated member of the Student Affairs Office |
| **Timeline** | Complete program design before the next academic year begins and promote it to the target group in the first week of semester |
| **Expected outcome** | Dropout among non-traditional students may decrease by about 20%, covering 30-50 students |
| **Required resources** | Assign one dedicated advisor or allocate 20% of an existing advisor’s workload; online resource development is expected to cost about CNY 30,000 |

---

### Recommendation 5: Establish a quarterly review mechanism for the warning system

| Item | Content |
|------|------|
| **Priority** | Low, but important for long-term effectiveness |
| **Action** | At the end of each semester, hold a review meeting involving the Academic Affairs Office, Student Affairs Office, and Finance Office to compare warning lists with actual dropout outcomes, evaluate intervention effects, and feed new data back into the system for continuous model improvement |
| **Owner** | Organized by the data analysis function of the Academic Affairs Office, or by an external data advisor if needed |
| **Timeline** | Fixed at the end of each semester, with the first review this semester |
| **Expected outcome** | Early-warning accuracy improves by 3-5 percentage points per year, and false negatives drop from 25% to below 15% within three years |
| **Required resources** | About half a day per meeting, 5-8 participants, and no additional budget required |

## 4. Risk Notes

### 4.1 System limitations - do not over-rely on the algorithm

**One out of every four dropout students may still not appear on the warning list.** The false negative rate is about 25%, meaning that if the university relies only on the system list, about 355 real high-risk students could still be missed. Advisors’ daily observation, peer feedback, and teaching staff reports remain essential complementary channels.

**The system cannot predict sudden events.** Family crises, major illness, and psychological emergencies are outside the system’s observable range. Traditional advisor-led care mechanisms should therefore remain in place rather than being replaced by algorithmic monitoring alone.

**The system is based on historical data and therefore has timeliness risk.** If admissions policy, tuition standards, or the macroeconomic environment change substantially, model performance may decline. The model should be refreshed at least once per academic year.

### 4.2 Ethical and privacy risks - non-negotiable red lines

| Risk type | Specific risk | Mitigation |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **Privacy leakage** | If student risk scores are exposed, this may cause discrimination or psychological harm | Warning lists should be accessible only to advisors and designated Student Affairs managers, and screenshots must not be shared |
| **Labeling harm** | Telling a student that “the system marked you as high risk” may create stress or even accelerate withdrawal | All intervention should be framed as routine care and not reveal risk scores |
| **Discriminatory decision-making** | Predictions must not be used for scholarship selection, awards, or other unrelated decisions | Create a clear data usage policy with accountability for misuse |
| **False alarms affecting low-risk students** | The system’s false positive rate is about 12%, meaning around 74 low-risk students may appear on the list | Advisors should use human judgment after conversations and should not apply mandatory measures to every flagged student |

### 4.3 Implementation challenges - prepare in advance

- **Advisor workload pressure:** Intensive conversations with 150 students will significantly increase short-term workload. It is advisable to postpone other non-urgent administrative tasks and provide appropriate performance recognition.
- **Cross-department collaboration barriers:** Data sharing between the Finance Office and Student Affairs Office may require permission adjustments and senior leadership support.
- **Student resistance:** Some students may feel uncomfortable being closely monitored. Advisors should receive communication training so that conversations can be conducted in a natural and non-judgmental way.
